Does AI really waste all our water?

🕑 Last updated June 13, 2026⏱ 6 min read📎 9 sources
The short version

AI's water use is real but small at national scale — U.S. golf courses use 3× more water than every data center on Earth combined. The legitimate concern is local: big facilities in already-stressed regions. Fight that, not a bad statistic.

01What you've heard

"AI is draining our water. Every ChatGPT query drinks a bottle of water, and data centers are sucking communities dry."

02What's actually true — the numbers

Pull any of these and they bring their source with them when you paste:

476B vs 148B gallons/yr
U.S. golf courses vs. all data centers worldwide — a 3.2× ratio.
Center for Irrigation Technology (USGA); EESI, 2023
43 golf courses
All of Google's global data centers (6.4B gal, 2023) equal the annual irrigation of just 43 golf courses.
Google 2023 Environmental Report
905M vs 29B gallons
In Maricopa County (2025), data centers used 905M gallons; golf courses used ~29 billion — 32× more.
Arizona reporting, 2025

The "bottle per query" figure comes from one 2023 estimate that bundles in electricity-generation water and has been stretched well past what its authors claimed.3

03Where the real concern is

The honest worry isn't national totals — it's local and regional. A large facility in a water-stressed county (parts of Arizona, Texas) can strain that specific aquifer, and siting decisions are often made with minimal public input. That's real, specific, and winnable — a different fight than "AI is an environmental catastrophe."4

Talking points — ready to paste

  • "Golf uses 3× more water than every data center on Earth — combined."
  • "The water issue is real, but it's local — not a reason to oppose AI, a reason to fight for smart siting rules."
  • "If we're serious about water, golf courses and alfalfa are a bigger fight than data centers."

04The progressive move

Don't cede the water issue to bad math. Push for what matters: public-interest siting rules, water-recycling and air-cooling mandates, and transparency on local draw — so host communities share the benefits, not just the costs.
